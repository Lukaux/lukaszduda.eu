Evaluating design ideas

Research-based prioritization method

Context

With a team full of ideas and limited development resources, it's crucial to carefully select projects for implementation. In response to this need, I proposed and implemented a method for evaluating design ideas, based on knowledge from research and analysis.

Key achievements

  • I created and maintain a UX repository that combines research and analysis with ideas for new projects.
  • I conducted 10+ ideation workshops that generate dozens of ideas for service improvements.
  • I designed and implemented a project evaluation method based on research and analysis.
  • I delivered 94 key insights that contributed to design and business decisions.

Process

Research & insights repository

While working on the email service, I conducted a significant amount of research and analysis. I created a repository for this research to provide a holistic view of all available user knowledge. The repository captures key findings and observations from each study. This allows anyone on the team to access key user and market insights at any time.

Project repository

I also maintain a project repository, which includes all existing ideas for service improvements. My team and I use the How Might We method to respond to most of my research and analysis, which generates many ideas for future projects and changes. I gather these ideas in one place, accessible to all team members. The ideas are then described and evaluated using the method described below.

Design idea evaluation method

Having a broad knowledge base from research and analysis, and a database of product improvement ideas, we can cross them. I developed a scoring method that allows us to prioritize ideas, starting from the most relevant. I evaluate potential improvements based on their ability to meet basic user needs, potential loyalizing factors, market factors, and potential costs and revenues.

Core needs criterion

I divide basic user needs into functionality, credibility, usability, and aesthetics. Addressing these needs helps lift users from the position of critics. Neglecting them will result in a decline in service ratings and the number of active users. I use experience radar to identify basic user problems and needs.

Loyalty drivers criterion

Loyalty factors (motivators) determine whether a user likes a service. Covering them allows users to move from a neutral position to a promoter position and provides reasons to log in more frequently. Neglecting them risks churn, even if the basics are taken care of. I look for motivators in functionality, credibility, usability, aesthetics, empathy, enjoyment, a sense of purpose, and engagement. To identify motivators, I examine users preferences for other services, as well as the emotions and slogans that resonate with them.

Market criterion

Market conditions are constantly changing. Competitive actions, legislative changes, and global trends impact every service. Identifying and addressing external factors allows us to improve our image, secure our future, and actively shape it. Neglecting external factors risks losing competitiveness and leading to a shift of users to more innovative services. I monitor competitive services, seeking market standards and opportunities for competitive advantage. I analyze global trends, identifying potential threats and opportunities to capitalize on.

Costs && revenue

In collaboration with the IT department, I initially estimate potential project costs. I consider implementation costs, maintenance costs, maintenance cuts, and relationships to other planned projects. In collaboration with the business department, I identify potential revenue opportunities. I also highlight the potential contribution to freemium and subscription models.

Practical application

I'm constantly expanding my database of projects assessed using the scoring method. I set the scoring to current business goals, controlling the weighting for each of the five criteria. As a team, we receive pre-filtered tasks to place on the roadmap. This method allows us to evaluate both minor fixes, larger projects, and sets of projects combined into service development directions. An additional advantage is that the described and visualized projects provide a brief for designers to prepare a highly detailed design.

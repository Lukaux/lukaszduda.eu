Email service research

Overview of my research and analysis

Context

While working as UX designer for an email service, I needed to gain knowledge about users and the email market. I independently conducted further research and analysis until this became my primary role, which I continue to perform today. Below, I present selected areas of my research.

Key achievements

  • I designed and conducted many surveys for a service with a multi-million active user base and gained useful insights.
  • I led the implementation of the NPS metric across all versions of the service and maintain it.
  • I combined multiple user information channels into a single database, providing new research perspectives.
  • I initiated and lead a project for user behavior measurement, contributing to the redesign of the information architecture.
  • I designed and implemented periodic quantitative-qualitative research method, supported by AI.
  • I created a competitors functionality index and implemented periodic reports on changes in competing services.
  • I prepared a report on global trends and legal regulations affecting the email market, which allowed us to discover new potential directions for the service's development.
  • I co-led a series of in-depth interviews on email service usage preferences, obtaining key insights for the service's development.

Process

User structure

First of all, I gathered basic information about the user structure. I relied on analytical and declarative data. I obtained the declarative data through surveys. I identified the dominant age groups and gender ratios. I learned what devices they used, why they had an account with us and how long they had been using it. This basic knowledge allowed us to better understand who we were designing for.

Product analytics

Meanwhile, I initiated a product analytics project. It involves counting views and clicks on selected interface elements, using native solutions and Firebase. I developed and maintain documentation, which I use to request measurement of individual elements for the Front-End, Android and iOS teams. Knowledge from this project allows us to better organize our information architecture.

NPS analysis

I manage the NPS score for all services covered by our project team. I ensure appropriate sampling on a daily basis. When necessary, I deepen this data by cross-referencing it with internal analytics. I examine how users rate us based on device, browser, gender, age, length of service use, and use of specific features.

Experience Radar

My main research task is running the Experience Radar. It is a tool I designed, for monitoring user opinions. I gather opinions from user comments from multiple sources, reaching approximately 20,000 per month. I built an AI-based model to analyze these opinions. You can read the Experience Radar case study here.

Survey research

Within the team, I've gained trust to independently conduct surveys, which allows me to regularly conduct further tests. I experiment with the format and scope of questions, depending on the knowledge we need at a given stage. Thanks to well-designed surveys, we better understand what changes users expect, what they value from other services, and why they choose our email service.

Competition monitoring

I constantly monitor competitor activities. Based on research, I've defined the services we consider as direct competition. I regularly analyze changes in these services, and compile my findings in a monthly report. I maintain an index of features found in other services. This allows us to understand which market standards we need to catch up on, and which areas we can build competitive advantages in.

Desk research

I contribute to the long-term development of the service. I search for global trends and analyze their potential impact on email market. These analyses include possible opportunities and threats, recommended development directions, and planned legislative changes relevant to the service.

Qualitative research

I co-led a series of in-depth interviews on how email services are used. We spoke with users about their daily work with email, methods of organizing messages, naming interface elements, approaches to paying for services, and much more. The findings from the interviews significantly changed our understanding of user perceptions, and influenced the scope of our future work.
